Why does Gavin Newsom love traffic so much?

His reasons are his own, and we can only speculate as to what they are. All we can say for sure is that the Governor has set an arbitrary policy that will add thousands of additional cars to California's roadways.

The policy is a return to office (RTO) mandate that requires all California state workers to work onsite at least 4 days per week without operational need. Many state workers have worked effectively from home 5 days per week for over four years, and in so doing have defrayed significant costs to California's taxpayers and the environment.

Until the RTO mandate, 37% of state employees worked primarily from home.1 Gavin's return-to-office mandate will force an estimated 90,000 state workers on the roads throughout California; that means we can expect over 30,000 more cars in Sacramento and over 7,000 in Los Angeles during peak commuting hours.2

Read About the Environmental Costs

California is already failing to meet its climate goals,3 and over 25% of the most polluted cities in the country are located in California.4

From 2021-2023, state telework policies prevented nearly 400,000 metric tons of carbon from being released into the atmosphere due to daily commutes.5

Based on the most recent month of Department of General Services' Tracking Telework data, the Governor's mandate for state workers to report to office locations at least 4 days per week is estimated to create a minimum of about 178,000 metric tons of carbon dioxide emissions over the course of a year.

Gavin failed to conduct an environmental impact assessment for the RTO policy as required by the California Environmental Quality Act.6 If he effectively ends telework for state workers by requiring employees to report to the office 5 days per week, the annual estimated CO2 emitted as a result of the policy could near 250 million metric tons.

Given the urgency of the climate emergency, we need to take as many measures as possible to prevent catastrophic climate change. Telework is one of many necessary tools in our toolkit to ward off climate change. California can't afford RTO.

Read About the Health Costs

Putting more cars on the road creates traffic, resulting in longer commute times.7 Gavin may love traffic, but more time spent in traffic means more exposure to hazardous pollutants: longer commute times are linked to higher individual dosage of chemicals identified as harmful in Proposition 65, such as benzene and formaldehyde.8

In addition to creating traffic, more cars on the road means there are more opportunities for collisions.9 Preventable deaths due to road injury have gone up in California over the past two decades,10 and there are motor vehicle crash hotspots near state office locations in Sacramento.11 California can't afford RTO.

Read About the Costs to Taxpayers

When more cars are on the road, pavement deteriorates more quickly, and roadways across California are already declining in quality.12 Sacramento roadways are no exception, and maintenance for road infrastructure in the city is critically underfunded.13 It will cost billions of dollars to fix the roadways, and taxpayers will foot the bill.12

Nearly half of state departments said state employees teleworking allowed them to reduce office space and save money on leases,14 and there were plans to turn some unneeded state offices into housing.15 A State Auditor's report estimated it would cost the state $225 million dollars per year to lease additional office space for the state to accommodate a 2 day in office requirement;16 the current 4 day policy is even more costly. California can't afford RTO.

Absent an operational reason to take 100% telework off the table for state employees, we can only assume that Gavin must love traffic, pollution, carbon emissions, and wasting everyone's time and money—or, at least, he must care more about making traffic worse than he does about meeting California's ambitious climate goals.

Help Gavin break up with traffic

Fortunately for Gavin, there is hope. A bill that would establish telework as the default work arrangement for state workers (AB 1729) is currently moving through the California legislature.17 The bill would re-establish the DGS Telework Dashboard to highlight annual savings attributable to telework.1 The bill passed the California assembly and is now moving through the senate. If you want to help Gavin break up with traffic, call or email your state senator and ask them to vote yes on AB 1729. And if you're a state worker, there are additional actions you can take to help Gavin break up with traffic above and beyond contacting your state senator.
